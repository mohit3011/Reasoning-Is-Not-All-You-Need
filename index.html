Abstract

Limited access to mental healthcare, extended wait times, and increasing capabilities of Large Language Models (LLMs) has led individuals to turn to LLMs for fulfilling their mental health needs. However, examining the multi-turn mental health conversation capabilities of LLMs remains under-explored. Existing evaluation frameworks typically focus on diagnostic accuracy and win-rates and often overlook alignment with patient-specific goals, values, and personalities required for meaningful conversations. To address this, we introduce MedAgent, a novel framework for synthetically generating realistic, multi-turn mental health sensemaking conversations and use it to create the Mental Health Sensemaking Dialogue (MHSD) dataset, comprising over 2,200 patient–LLM conversations. Additionally, we present MultiSenseEval, a holistic framework to evaluate the multi-turn conversation abilities of LLMs in healthcare settings using human-centric criteria. Our findings reveal that frontier reasoning models yield below-par performance for patient-centric communication and struggle at precise (“hard”) diagnostic capabilities with average accuracy of ∼31%. Additionally, we observed variation in model performance based on patient's persona and performance drop with increasing turns in the conversation. Our work provides a comprehensive synthetic data generation framework, a dataset and evaluation framework for assessing LLMs in multi-turn mental health conversations.

What's New

Three contributions, one end-to-end pipeline

A model-agnostic conversation generator, a 2,284-conversation dataset, and a six-axis evaluation framework grounded in clinical-communication theory.

MedAgent Framework

A model-agnostic, two-actor pipeline that grounds patient personas in real clinical case studies and structures sensemaker behavior over a five-stage clinical-communication scaffold.

MHSD Dataset

2,284 synthetic patient–LLM conversations covering 181 MedQA-derived mental-health case studies replayed across five Big Five trait variations (one trait dialed High at a time), two health-literacy levels, three intents, and three goals.

MultiSenseEval

Six-axis evaluation: Perceived Susceptibility, Perceived Severity, Perceived Benefits, Diagnostic Accuracy (Hard / Soft), Flow & Correctness, and Readability (SMOG). Validated by domain-expert annotators.

Framework

Two cooperating actors, grounded in clinical theory

Patient personas are dialed via Big-Five traits, health literacy, intent, and goals. Sensemaker behavior is organized around the 6-function model of medical communication.

Module 1

Patient Actor LLM

The patient is built from atomic medical facts distilled from MedQA case studies, then layered with a structured persona. Each turn retrieves only the facts relevant to the sensemaker's prompt, then re-styles them through the persona's tone.

  • Personality — Big Five trait dialed one at a time to avoid contradictory mixes.
  • Health Literacy — Basic or Advanced, shaping vocabulary and detail.
  • Intent — what the user came for (e.g. interpretation, lifestyle advice).
  • Goal — Comfort-focused, Maintain/Improve Function, or Life Extension.
Module 2

Sensemaker Actor LLM

The sensemaker organizes each conversation across five clinical-communication stages, grounded in the 6-function model of medical communication (de Haes & Bensing; King & Hoppe): Fostering the Relationship → Gathering Information → Providing Information → Decision Making → Responding to Emotions. A hard cap of 5 turns per stage prevents stagnation.

  • Next Stage Determination — advances or holds based on accumulated facts.
  • Fact Extraction & Matching — incrementally builds a patient memory.
  • Message Generation — produces 3 stage-appropriate candidates.
  • Redundancy Selector — filters semantically duplicate replies.
Personas

Five Big-Five dials, five conversation styles

Each case study is replayed five times — one personality trait dialed High at a time — yielding stable, internally-consistent simulated patients.

Openness

Imagination, curiosity, comfort with novel ideas.
High: Asks why/how, explores alternatives.
Low: Prefers familiar terms, concrete instructions.

Conscientiousness

Organization, self-discipline, goal-orientation.
High: Brings detailed symptom logs, seeks precise steps.
Low: Vague histories, prefers simplified plans.

Extraversion

Sociability, talkativeness, expressiveness.
High: Rich detail, small talk, frequent questions.
Low: Succinct answers, prefers structured questioning.

Agreeableness

Cooperation, trust, prosocial orientation.
High: Readily agrees; may conceal doubts.
Low: Challenges recommendations, negotiates choices.

Neuroticism

Emotional reactivity, anxiety, mood instability.
High: Focuses on worries, seeks reassurance.
Low: Calm, handles technical explanations easily.

A six-axis lens on patient-centric conversation

Three patient-centric communication metrics from the Health Belief Model, plus diagnostic accuracy, conversational flow, and readability. LLM-as-a-judge scores were validated against expert annotators.

Patient-Centric
AXIS 01

Perceived Susceptibility

Does the sensemaker help the patient understand their personal risk of the condition? 1–4 Likert.

Patient-Centric
AXIS 02

Perceived Severity

Does it communicate the seriousness and likely consequences clearly and appropriately?

Patient-Centric
AXIS 03

Perceived Benefits

Does it convey concrete, personalized benefits of recommended actions or treatments?

Diagnostic
AXIS 04

Diagnostic Accuracy

Hard = exact match to MedQA ground truth. Soft = broader category match. Free-text, no multiple choice.

Conversational Flow
AXIS 05

Flow & Correctness

End-to-end coverage and logical ordering of the five clinical stages, judged by an LLM rubric.

Readability
AXIS 06

Readability (SMOG)

Grade-level readability index — health orgs recommend SMOG 6–8 for patient materials.

Human evaluation agreement

Across two rounds of human review (graduate annotators and Prolific-recruited medical/healthcare experts), the LLM-as-a-judge scores correlated 89% with humans for Hard Diagnostic Accuracy, 93% for Soft, 85–88% for the three Health-Belief-Model metrics, and 79% for Conversation Flow & Correctness.

Findings

Frontier reasoning models underperform on patient-centric care

Both OpenAI o1 and DeepSeek-R1 score below the “Good Performance” threshold on every patient-centric communication axis. Diagnostic accuracy drops over 50% when matched exactly, and both models lose ground as conversations grow longer.

Agreeable patients are easier — for both models

Among advanced-literacy patients, R1 reaches its best patient-centric scores on Agreeableness (2.67 Susceptibility, 2.81 Severity, 3.12 Benefits). The same patient with the Neuroticism trait dialed high drops these by 7.1% / 7.5% / 7.4% — a measurable persona bias toward prosocial, cooperative interlocutors.

Health literacy / readability trade-off

Advanced-literacy patients get marginally better patient-centric communication (+2.77% for o1; +0.63% for R1), but the replies get harder to read — SMOG rises 5.84% (o1) and 3.29% (R1). Even at their easiest, both models exceed the 6–8 SMOG range recommended for patient-facing materials (o1: 11.50, R1: 13.33).

Longer conversations, worse care

R1's Perceived Susceptibility drops 12.83%, Perceived Severity 6.71%, and Hard Diagnostic Accuracy 29.62% as sensemaker turns increase from 5 to 10–15. o1 follows the same trajectory (−4.8% / −7.99% / −16.21%). Reasoning quality doesn't sustain itself across multi-turn clinical conversations.

Context

Where this sits in the literature

Med-PaLM and the GPT series have demonstrated strong single-turn performance on medical benchmarks, but they remain vulnerable to hallucination, cross-lingual disparities, sociocultural bias, and expert misalignment. Recent multi-turn work — MediQ, APP, and patient-simulator frameworks — has begun to probe iterative diagnostic conversation, but largely treats evaluation as classification or win-rate, and stops at the point of diagnosis. Our work targets the open-ended sensemaking arc: how well an LLM helps a patient understand their situation, not just label it, across personality, literacy, and conversation length.

Responsibility

Ethics & Limitations

  • Not a clinical tool. MHSD is a research dataset for evaluating LLM conversational behavior; it is not validated for diagnosis, treatment, or any clinical decision-making.
  • Synthetic-data caveats. Patient actors are LLM-generated and may under-represent the full variability of real patients, including comorbidities, cultural context, and lived experience.
  • Persona dial is binary. Big-Five traits are varied one-at-a-time on a binary High/Low scale to maintain internal consistency. Realistic personalities are continuous and correlated.
  • Two reasoning models. Findings are reported for OpenAI o1 and DeepSeek-R1 as sensemakers; behavior of other frontier or future models is not guaranteed to follow the same patterns.
  • SMOG is one of many readability lenses. Health-organization guidance (6–8) is a useful target but does not capture cultural appropriateness, tone, or trauma-informed phrasing.
